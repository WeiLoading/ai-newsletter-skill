Do not restrict discovery to fixed accounts, keywords, or subreddits. Search broadly within the focus areas below.

## Focus Areas

1. Major AI companies: models, products, capabilities, APIs, pricing, availability, acquisitions, partnerships, research, suspension, and deprecation.
2. AI coding and agents: coding agents, browser agents, computer use, multi-agent systems, harnesses, skills, plugins, sandboxes, workspaces, memory, evaluation, and observability.
3. Multimodality: image, video, speech, realtime interaction, world models, and multimodal understanding or generation.
4. Model systems: long context, tool calling, RAG, routing, inference, quantization, local or on-device models, serving infrastructure, speed, cost, and token efficiency.
5. New paradigms: repeated new terms, frameworks, product patterns, development methods, and AI-native workflows.
6. Independent products and open source: startups, individual developers, experimental tools, vertical AI, and developer infrastructure.
7. Product signals: user pain, unmet demand, workflow changes, micro-SaaS opportunities, safety, auditing, governance, and cost control.

These are discovery directions, not a closed keyword list.

## Selection

Prefer:

- Official announcements and primary material.
- Topics discussed across multiple communities.
- Posts by official accounts, researchers, core developers, or recognized domain experts.
- Real experiments exposing capability, limitations, cost, or workflow impact.
- High-discussion items and credible early signals with product or technical value.

Exclude:

- Obvious advertising.
- Generic non-AI technology news.
- Unsupported emotional arguments.
- Reposts with no additional information.
- Untraceable secondary claims.
- Items without a verified original URL.
- Low-value material used only to meet a count.

## Discovery Fallbacks

When direct access fails, continue instead of dropping the source:

1. Open the source’s index, article page, RSS, sitemap, changelog, or public API.
2. Use search engines to discover exact original URLs.
3. Use public indexes and platform APIs for metadata.
4. Follow original links cited by HN, GitHub, official blogs, or reputable public pages.
5. Cross-check dates through page metadata or stable identifiers.

For X:

- Require a concrete `x.com/{handle}/status/{id}` or equivalent original status URL.
- Public search indexes and HN-linked statuses may be used for discovery.
- A Snowflake status ID may be decoded to verify creation time.
- Never invent a status ID, author, text, timestamp, or engagement count.

For Hacker News:

- Link `https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=...`.
- Summarize the HN discussion angle, not only the linked article.
- Balance highly discussed stories with credible early Show HN or Ask HN signals.

For Reddit:

- Link a concrete Reddit post.
- Summarize the post and meaningful comment themes.
- Expand beyond familiar subreddits when the day’s topics require it.

## Truth Boundary

- Search snippets discover candidates; they do not independently prove facts.
- Prefer original publication time over index time.
- Mark reasonable interpretation as analysis, not confirmed fact.
- When sources conflict, use the source closest to the original event.
- Omit uncertain facts instead of filling gaps.
